Why walnut blasting

Why modern engines suffer from carbon build-up

On direct-injection engines the fuel is injected straight into the combustion chamber ‚Äì not over the back of the valves like older port-injection designs. That means there is nothing to wash away oily vapour and exhaust gas recirculation deposits that cling to the intake valves and ports, slowly choking the engine.

Typical symptoms that improve after a proper clean

  • ‚ö° Hesitation and flat spots
    Restricted airflow and uneven mixture cause a ‚Äúdead‚Äù feeling when you try to accelerate, especially from low RPM.
  • üõë Rough idle and cold-start misfires
    Carbon build-up disturbs airflow around the valves and can upset mixture enough to trigger misfire counts and warning lights.
  • ‚õΩ Poor fuel economy
    The ECU ends up adding more fuel to compensate for poor breathing, so MPG drops despite similar driving.
  • üèÅ Performance not matching the spec
    The engine no longer feels like the power figure on paper ‚Äì particularly noticeable on tuned and performance models.
Why this method

Walnut blasting vs ‚Äúmiracle‚Äù carbon cleaning sprays & HHO machines

We‚Äôre often asked about HHO carbon cleaning, fuel additives and intake sprays. These methods can sometimes freshen up an engine that is already fairly clean ‚Äì but they struggle to remove the thick, baked-on deposits you actually see on direct-injection intake valves.

Method How it works Risks & effectiveness
Walnut blasting (our method) Crushed walnut shell media is blasted at the intake valves and ports with the manifold removed and the valves closed on the cylinder being worked on. Safest and most effective way to physically remove heavy carbon deposits from valves & ports when done correctly. No harsh chemicals in the cylinders and no need to run the engine while cleaning.
HHO & ‚Äúgas‚Äù carbon cleaning A machine feeds a gas mixture (often hydrogen/oxygen) through the intake while the engine idles, aiming to loosen deposits as it burns. Often too weak to shift thick deposits on intake valves. Any material that does come loose has to go through the engine and exhaust, which isn‚Äôt ideal.
Fuel additives Chemicals added to the tank to clean injectors and combustion chambers as they pass through with the fuel. Can help injectors & combustion chambers, but they never touch the back of the intake valves on direct-injection engines ‚Äì exactly where the problem is.
Intake sprays / chemical soaks Solvents sprayed into the intake or left to soak on valves through access ports or the throttle body. Can soften light deposits but struggle with heavy build-up. There is a risk of softened lumps breaking away and being ingested if not controlled.

Walnut blasting is currently the most controlled and consistently effective way to restore heavily carboned intake valves and ports without risking damage to engine components.
